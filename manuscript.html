Exiting the Hegemony

An Autoethnographic Study of Zionist Formation and Liberation in American Civil Society

Derron S. Borders  ·  Kansas State University

Under review · Journal for the Critical Study of Zionism

Abstract

This paper applies Antonio Gramsci’s theory of hegemony, Paulo Freire’s concept of conscientização, and Mezirow’s transformative learning theory to analyze, through autoethnographic method, how I was formed as a subject of Zionist hegemony in the United States and how I exited that formation. Drawing on Gramsci’s distinction between consent and coercion, I trace the civil society institutions, specifically Christian Zionist theology and Holocaust education as a curricular vehicle, through which my consent for settler colonialism in Palestine was manufactured. I then document how relationships across difference cracked the hegemonic frame through what Daloz would call constructive engagement with otherness, and how the coercive apparatus activated in response, culminating in continued professional retaliation.

The analysis identifies a structural asymmetry in American K–12 education: Holocaust education relies predominantly on literary and affective modalities, producing identification with the only “acceptable tragedy,” while slavery is taught in the historical register, producing cognitive distance. Indigenous genocide is largely absent from K–12 curricula. I argue that this curricular selectivity is not pedagogical happenstance but hegemonic production operating through disciplinary genre.
